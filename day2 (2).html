Using AI Prompts to Build Websites That Don't Look AI-Made

Day 2  ·  March 29, 2026

🔗 Today's reference: MotionSites.ai — a library of premium prompts for generating animated website hero sections. Think of it as a prompt recipe book built specifically for web design.

Okay so I'll be honest — when I first heard "use AI to generate a website," I rolled my eyes a little. Because you know what AI-generated websites look like. Dark background. Purple gradient. Roboto font. A floating card with a fake dashboard screenshot. Every. Single. Time.

But then I came across MotionSites.ai and the examples there were... actually good? Like, genuinely good. The kind of good where you'd assume a designer spent two days on it. So I started digging into why — and it all comes down to the prompt.

What MotionSites Actually Is

MotionSites isn't a website builder. No editor, no drag and drop, no templates to fill in. It's purely a library of prompts — each one crafted to produce a specific type of animated website hero section when you paste it into an AI tool like Claude, Bolt, or v0.

The hero section is the very top of a webpage — the big headline, the one-liner below it, and the "Get Started" button. Visitors decide in about 3 seconds whether to scroll or close the tab. MotionSites exists entirely to make that 3-second window count.

The prompts aren't just descriptions. They're full design specifications — colors, fonts, animations, spacing, and what to avoid — all written before a single line of code is generated.

The site organizes prompts by category — Agency, SaaS, Portfolio, Fintech, Web3, Landing Page. Each has a live animated preview and a ready-to-copy prompt. Paste it into your AI tool, get working HTML/CSS back. That's the whole workflow.

Why "Just Describe It" Doesn't Work

The AI gives you the most statistically average result it can, based on millions of websites it has seen. And the average website is forgettable.

Ask for a cybersecurity site — dark mode, terminal green text, hacker in a hoodie. Ask for a portfolio — dark background, neon accent, a project card grid identical to 40,000 others. This isn't the AI being lazy. It's just doing what averages do.

The solution isn't a better AI — it's a better prompt. Specifically, one that defines the design system upfront and explicitly bans the clichés the AI would otherwise reach for.

❌ What most people write Make me a website for a cybersecurity company. Dark theme, looks professional.
✅ What actually works --bg:#0B1120 --accent:#0ECEAA. Font: DM Sans 700. Blurred teal orb drifts in bg. Text fades up on load. NEGATIVE PROMPT: no terminal green, no glitch effects, no hoodies.

The Full Prompt Structure

After going through a lot of MotionSites examples, they all follow the same order. And the order matters — each layer builds on the one before it: 

# 1. Design system first — always
CSS variables for every color. Font names. Not "dark blue" — #0B1120.
Not "modern font" — DM Sans, weight 700.

# 2. Layout and content
What sections exist. Exact words for headlines and subtext.
Not "standard layout" — "centered, headline above subtext above two buttons."

# 3. Real assets (the piece most people skip)
Do you have a video, a Spline 3D scene, a Lottie file, real images?
Reference them here. The AI will wire them in correctly.

# 4. Animation behaviour
Describe it plainly. "Slow floating blurred orb" or
"underline draws in left to right." Not just "make it animated."

# 5. Negative prompt
Everything the AI must NOT produce.
This is where most prompts fall short — more on this next.

Negative Prompting — The Proper Term

We've been calling it "anti-rules" but it has a real technical name: negative prompting.

The term comes from image generation models like Stable Diffusion and Midjourney, where you pass a dedicated field of things you don't want in the output — blurry, low quality, extra fingers, watermark — and the model actively steers away from them during generation. The exact same principle carries over to AI web design prompts.

Without a negative prompt, the AI fills every gap with its defaults. And its defaults for tech/security sites are almost always:

  • Terminal green #00FF00 text
  • Glitch / scanline effects
  • Matrix-style rain animation
  • Stock photos of hooded figures
  • Generic Inter or Roboto fonts
  • Purple gradient on white background

A properly written negative prompt looks like this:

NEGATIVE PROMPT:
Do not use terminal green (#00FF00), glitch effects, scanline overlays,
Matrix rain animations, stock photos of hooded figures, aggressive neon
colors, generic sans-serif fallbacks, or dark grunge textures.
Awwwards-level quality only — nothing that looks like a free template.
💡 Think of it less as a rule and more as taste in writing — you're telling the AI which aesthetic neighbourhood you refuse to live in. The more specific you are here, the less generic the output.

Including Real Assets in Your Prompt

This is the piece most tutorials leave out entirely, and it's a big reason results feel flat. When you give AI a prompt with no actual assets — no images, no video, no 3D — it fills everything with placeholders. Grey boxes. Gradient blobs pretending to be visuals. The result looks like a mockup, not a real site.

The fix: bring your own assets and reference them directly in the prompt.

🎭Spline 3DFree 3D scenes from spline.design — use the embed/publish URL
✨LottieJSON animations from LottieFiles — reference the CDN link
🖼️Your ImagesScreenshots, product shots, photography — describe placement too

When you include real assets, the AI stops guessing and starts building around something concrete. The jump in quality is immediately noticeable — even one real image makes a site feel finished rather than generated.

Case Study — CipherShield (Ethical Hacking Community)

The task: a landing page for a professional ethical hacking community offering pen testing, red team operations, and compliance audits. The constraint — no hoodie aesthetic, no glitch, no Matrix rain. These are real professionals and the site needs to say that.

Design direction: McKinsey for cybersecurity. Deep navy, electric teal accent (same palette as Stripe and Linear), DM Sans headings, one slow blurred orb in the background and nothing else moving. 

Build a full-screen HTML/CSS landing hero for "CipherShield" —
a professional ethical hacking and pen testing community.

Design system:
--bg: #0B1120   --text: #F5F7FA   --accent: #0ECEAA
Font: DM Sans (Google Fonts), headline 62px weight 700, tracking -0.02em

Hero content:
Headline:    "We Break In So Others Can't"
Subtext:     "Certified penetration testing and red team operations
              for enterprises that can't afford to be wrong."
Primary CTA: "Book a Free Scoping Call"
Ghost CTA:   "View Case Studies"
Trust line:  "200+ Clients Protected  ·  14,000+ Vulnerabilities Found"

Animation:
- Blurred teal ellipse (400x300px, 20% opacity) drifts slowly on a 20s CSS loop
- Hero text staggered fade-up: headline 0ms, subtext 120ms, CTAs 240ms
- Primary button: teal glow on hover + scale(1.02)

NEGATIVE PROMPT:
Do not use terminal green (#00FF00), glitch/scanline effects,
Matrix rain, stock photos of hoodies, dark grunge textures,
aggressive neon. Output clean HTML + CSS, no JS frameworks.

Why Your Prompt Might Not Have Worked

If you tried a prompt and the result didn't look right, here are the honest reasons why — not vague advice, but the actual failure modes:

1
You didn't write a negative prompt This is the single most common reason outputs look generic. Even one line like "no terminal green, no glitch effects" makes a measurable difference. Without it, the AI defaults to whatever pattern it knows best for that category.
2
The prompt was too short Vague prompts produce vague output. If your prompt was under 80 words, the AI had too many gaps to fill in on its own — and filled them with defaults. Specificity is the whole point.
3
No real assets CSS gradient blobs and placeholder boxes will always make a site feel unfinished, no matter how good the layout is. Even one real image or a short looping video changes the perception completely.
4
The font didn't load If you specified a Google Font and the tool didn't add the import link, the browser falls back to a system font and the whole feel of the site shifts. Always check the <head> of the generated code to confirm the <link> tag is there.
5
Wrong tool for the prompt Different tools follow instructions differently. Bolt.new and Claude tend to follow precise design specs closely. Some other tools are more "creative" with your instructions — meaning they quietly ignore parts they find inconvenient. If the output looks off, try the same prompt in a different tool before editing the prompt itself.
AI doesn't produce bad websites because it's bad at design. It produces bad websites because most people give it bad prompts. Specify the hex codes. Name the fonts. Reference real assets. Write the negative prompt. That's genuinely the whole secret.
