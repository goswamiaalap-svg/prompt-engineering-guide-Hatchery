Using AI Prompts to Build Websites That Don't Look AI-Made

Day 2  ·  March 29, 2026

🔗 Today's reference: MotionSites.ai — a library of premium prompts for generating animated website hero sections. Think of it as a prompt recipe book built specifically for web design.

Okay so I'll be honest — when I first heard "use AI to generate a website," I rolled my eyes a little. Because you know what AI-generated websites look like. Dark background. Purple gradient. Roboto font. A floating card with a fake dashboard screenshot. Every. Single. Time.

But then I came across MotionSites.ai and the examples there were... actually good? Like, genuinely good. The kind of good where you'd assume a designer spent two days on it. So I started digging into why — and it all comes down to the prompt.

What MotionSites Actually Is

MotionSites isn't a website builder. There's no editor, no drag and drop, no templates to fill in. It's purely a library of prompts — each one crafted to produce a specific type of animated website hero section when you paste it into an AI tool like Claude, Bolt, or v0.

The hero section, if you're not familiar, is the very top of a webpage — the big headline, the one-liner below it, and the "Get Started" button. It's the most important part of any site because visitors decide in about 3 seconds whether to scroll or close the tab. MotionSites exists entirely to make that 3-second window count.

The prompts aren't just descriptions. They're full design specifications — colors, fonts, animations, spacing, and what to avoid — all written out before a single line of code is generated.

The site organizes prompts into categories: Agency, SaaS, Portfolio, Fintech, Web3, Landing Page. Each one has a live preview you can look at and a prompt you can copy straight from the page. Paste it into your AI tool of choice, hit enter, and get working HTML/CSS back. That's the whole workflow.

Why "Just Describe It" Doesn't Work

Here's the thing nobody tells you when they say "just ask AI to build your website." The AI is going to give you the most statistically average result it can, based on millions of websites it's seen. And the average website is pretty forgettable.

Ask for a cybersecurity site — you're getting dark mode, terminal green text, and a hacker in a hoodie. Ask for a portfolio — dark background, neon accent, and a grid of project cards that looks identical to 40,000 other portfolios. This isn't the AI being lazy. It's just doing what averages do.

What MotionSites figured out is that the solution isn't a better AI — it's a better prompt. Specifically, one that defines the design system upfront and explicitly bans the clichés the AI would otherwise reach for.

❌ What most people write Make me a cool website for a cybersecurity company. Dark theme, looks professional.
✅ What MotionSites prompts look like --bg:#0B1120 --accent:#0ECEAA --text:#F5F7FA. Font: DM Sans 700. 62px headline. Blurred teal orb drifts slowly in bg (CSS keyframes). Text staggered fade-up on load. NO terminal green, no glitch effects, no Matrix rain.

See the difference? The second one specifies exact hex codes, names the font, describes the animation in plain English, and — this is the part most people skip — explicitly tells the AI what not to do. That last part alone cuts out 90% of the generic output.

The Structure Behind Every Good Prompt

After going through a bunch of MotionSites examples I noticed they all follow the same pattern. Not in a rigid template way, but the information is always in the same order — and that order matters: 

# 1. Design system first — always
CSS variables for every color. Font names. Don't say "dark blue." Say #0B1120.
Don't say "modern font." Say DM Sans, weight 700.

# 2. Layout and content
What sections exist. Exact words for headlines and subtext.
"Centered hero, headline above subtext above two buttons" — not "standard layout."

# 3. Animation
Describe it the way you'd describe it to a person.
"Slow floating blurred orb" or "underline draws in left to right."
Not just "make it animated."

# 4. Anti-rules (the secret weapon)
NO terminal green. NO glitch effects. NO hoodie stock photos.
NO purple gradients. List every cliché for that site type and ban it explicitly.
💡 The anti-rules section feels weird to write at first — like, why tell the AI what not to do? But this is genuinely the most important part. Try running a prompt with and without it. The difference is immediate.

Let's Build Something — Ethical Hacking Community Site

The task: design a site for a professional ethical hacking community offering pen testing, red team operations, and compliance audits. The catch — no generic hoodie-hacker look, no glitch effects, no Matrix rain. These are real professionals and the site needs to reflect that.

The design direction I landed on: think McKinsey, but for cybersecurity. Deep navy background. Electric teal accent — the same palette Stripe and Linear use, actually. DM Sans for headings because it's sharp without being aggressive. One slow-moving blurred orb in the background for depth, nothing else moving.

The prompt

Build a full-screen HTML/CSS landing hero for "CipherShield" —
a professional ethical hacking and pen testing community.

Design system:
--bg: #0B1120   --text: #F5F7FA   --accent: #0ECEAA
Font: DM Sans (Google Fonts), headline 62px weight 700, tracking -0.02em

Hero content:
Headline:    "We Break In So Others Can't"
Subtext:     "Certified penetration testing and red team operations
              for enterprises that can't afford to be wrong."
Primary CTA: "Book a Free Scoping Call"
Ghost CTA:   "View Case Studies"
Trust line:  "200+ Clients Protected  ·  14,000+ Vulnerabilities Found"

Animation:
- Blurred teal ellipse (400x300px, 20% opacity) drifts slowly
  across the background on a 20s CSS loop
- Hero text staggered fade-up: headline 0ms, subtext 120ms, CTAs 240ms
- Primary button: teal glow on hover + scale(1.02)

DO NOT use: terminal green (#00FF00), glitch/scanline effects,
Matrix rain, stock photos of hoodies, dark grunge textures,
aggressive neon. Output clean HTML + CSS, no JS frameworks.

The headline "We Break In So Others Can't" is doing a lot of work here. It's confident without being threatening, immediately explains what the community does, and doesn't sound generated. The trust line below the CTAs answers "why should I trust you?" before anyone has to wonder.

And a Portfolio — Because the Defaults Here Are Especially Bad

Portfolio sites are where bad AI defaults hurt the most. I described "a developer portfolio" to a few people and every single one said: dark background, bright accent, project cards in a grid. Which is fine! But it also describes about a million other portfolios.

The move here is to give the AI a person to design for, not just a job title. Background, vibe, what they care about. The design decisions flow naturally from that.

The prompt

Personal portfolio hero for Arjun Mehta, UX Designer from Jaipur, India.

Personality brief:
Warm, curious, craft-forward. His work blends Indian design traditions
with modern digital product design. Nothing cold or overly techy about him.

Design system:
--bg: #FDFAF5   --text: #1A1A1A   --accent: #D4622A
Font-heading: Playfair Display · Font-body: Lato (both from Google Fonts)

Layout:
Left: headline → one-liner subtext → two CTA buttons
Right: circular avatar placeholder filled with warm terracotta gradient

Headline: "Designing experiences that feel human."
Sub:      "UX Designer crafting digital products rooted in empathy,
           simplicity, and culture."
CTAs:     "See My Work"  /  "Let's Talk" (ghost button)

Animation:
- Decorative underline under headline draws in (width 0 to 100%, 0.6s ease)
- Avatar fades in from the right with a 0.4s delay

DO NOT use: dark mode, neon colors, cold blue palette,
generic "Full Stack Developer" headline, techy grid layouts.
Responsive HTML + CSS only.

Notice how the personality brief ("warm, Indian craft traditions, nothing cold or techy") shapes every design decision — terracotta accent, Playfair Display headings, cream background. None of those choices are spelled out explicitly in the design system section; they follow naturally from the brief. That's the whole point of writing it that way.

The takeaway from today: AI doesn't produce bad websites because it's bad at design. It produces bad websites because most people give it bad prompts. Specify the hex codes. Name the fonts. Describe the animation. Ban the clichés. That's genuinely the whole secret.
